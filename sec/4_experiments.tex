\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate our proposed frequency-aware objectness calibration framework on both in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OoD) datasets. 

For ID evaluation, we use the Pascal VOC 2012 validation split. For OoD evaluation, we construct a proximal OoD dataset using Caltech-256 by selecting visually similar but semantically mismatched categories relative to VOC classes.

We perform inference using a finetuned YOLO-based detector and apply our calibration method as a post-processing step without modifying model parameters.

All experiments are conducted with a confidence threshold of $\tau = 0.25$.

%---------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\paragraph{Hallucination Rate (HR).}
We define hallucination rate as the fraction of images that produce at least one detection above a confidence threshold:

\begin{equation}
\text{HR} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}\left( \exists\, d \in \mathcal{D}_i \;:\; s_d > \tau \right)
\end{equation}

where $N$ is the number of images, $\mathcal{D}_i$ denotes detections in image $i$, $s_d$ is the confidence score, and $\tau$ is the threshold.

\paragraph{False Positive Count.}
We compute the total number of detections across the dataset:

\begin{equation}
\text{FP} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} |\mathcal{D}_i|
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Reduction Percentage.}
To quantify the effectiveness of calibration, we compute:

\begin{equation}
\text{Reduction}(\%) = 
\frac{\text{FP}_{\text{raw}} - \text{FP}_{\text{cal}}}
{\text{FP}_{\text{raw}}} \times 100
\end{equation}

\subsection{Ablation Analysis}

We analyze the contribution of individual components through a progressive calibration strategy. Frequency-based suppression alone reduces hallucinated detections by attenuating responses in high-texture regions. However, this effect is limited due to the persistence of overconfident predictions.

By incorporating objectness-aware modulation, the model further penalizes detections with inherently unstable confidence, leading to a substantial improvement in both false positive count and hallucination rate.

This demonstrates that frequency and objectness cues provide complementary signals for mitigating OoD-induced hallucinations.

%---------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Analysis of Confidence Calibration}

We analyze the distributional shift in detection confidence before and after calibration.

Let $\mathcal{S}_{\text{raw}}$ and $\mathcal{S}_{\text{cal}}$ denote confidence score distributions. We evaluate:

\begin{equation}
\Delta \mu = \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{S}_{\text{raw}}] - \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{S}_{\text{cal}}]
\end{equation}

Empirically, we observe $\Delta \mu > 0$, indicating a consistent reduction in mean confidence after calibration. This reflects suppression of overconfident predictions in OoD regions.

%---------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Frequency-Objectness Correlation}

To further validate our hypothesis, we analyze the relationship between frequency energy and objectness scores.

Given frequency values $f_i$ and objectness scores $o_i$, we examine their joint distribution:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C} = \{(f_i, o_i)\}_{i=1}^{M}
\end{equation}

We observe a positive correlation between $f_i$ and $o_i$, indicating that high-frequency regions tend to produce disproportionately high objectness scores. This supports the presence of frequency-induced bias in detector behavior.

%---------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Discussion}

Our results demonstrate that:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Frequency-aware calibration reduces spurious detections caused by textured regions.
    \item Objectness-aware suppression further stabilizes predictions by penalizing overconfident outputs.
    \item The combined approach achieves up to $27.03\%$ reduction in false positives and improves hallucination rates.
\end{itemize}

Importantly, these improvements are achieved without retraining or modifying the detector, making the method suitable for real-time and resource-constrained applications.
